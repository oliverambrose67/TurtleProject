Gopher Tortoise

Gopherus polyphemus

An Endangered Keystone Species in Alabama

A gopher tortoise emerging from its burrow.

About the Species

The Gopher Tortoise is a keystone species, meaning over 350 other species benefit from the burrows it digs. These burrows can be up to 40 feet long and provide shelter from predators and extreme temperatures.

They are long-lived reptiles, with some living over 60 years in the wild. Their high-domed upper shell, or carapace, is typically dark brown to gray.

It is listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act primarily due to habitat loss and degradation of the longleaf pine ecosystem.

Habitat & Alabama Range

Gopher tortoises thrive in well-drained, sandy soils found in longleaf pine forests, sandhills, and coastal dunes. These soils are crucial for digging their extensive burrows.

An open canopy with abundant sunlight is essential, as it promotes the growth of low-lying herbaceous plants that make up the majority of their diet.

In Alabama, they are primarily found in the southern counties, especially south of the Black Belt region. Mobile, Baldwin, Washington, and Covington counties have significant populations.

The sandy, half-moon shaped apron at a burrow entrance is a key indicator of an active burrow.

Nesting & Reproduction

Nesting occurs from May to July. The female digs a nest cavity in a sunny location, often in the sandy apron of her burrow.

She lays a clutch of about 5-8 brittle, white eggs that are roughly the size and shape of a ping-pong ball.

Incubation takes 80-110 days, and the hatchlings emerge in late summer or early fall. Nests and hatchlings are highly vulnerable to predators like raccoons, fire ants, and foxes.

Locomotion & Tracking

Gopher tortoises move slowly but deliberately. Their front feet are spade-like and adapted for powerful digging, while their back feet are elephantine or club-like, providing stability. The tracks they leave in sandy soil are distinctive and can be used for population monitoring.

Distinctive tracks leading to a burrow. Notice the drag mark from the shell (plastron).

A closer view. The front feet leave a comma-shaped print, while the rear feet leave a rounder impression.

For your AI Model: Look for these key features: parallel sets of prints, a potential center line from the plastron dragging, and the half-moon shape of the burrow entrance nearby. The sandy soil provides an excellent medium for capturing clear images for training data.
