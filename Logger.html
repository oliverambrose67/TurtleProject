Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Caretta caretta

The Loggerhead is named for its large head, which supports powerful jaw muscles, allowing it to crush hard-shelled prey like conchs and crabs.

Habitat & Alabama Range

Loggerheads are found in temperate and tropical regions of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They inhabit a wide range of environments, from coastal bays and estuaries to the deep open ocean.

In Alabama:

They are primarily found in the coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico. Nesting sites are crucial and are located on Alabama's sandy beaches, particularly in Baldwin County and on the Fort Morgan Peninsula, including areas like the Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge.

Nesting Habits

Female Loggerheads return to the general areas where they were born to lay their own eggs. They typically nest at night, emerging from the ocean to dig a flask-shaped nest in the sand above the high-tide line.

  • Clutch Size: An average of 100-120 eggs per nest.
  • Frequency: A female may lay 3-5 nests (or more) in a single season, nesting every 2-4 years.
  • Incubation: Eggs incubate for about 60 days. The temperature of the sand determines the sex of the hatchlings!

Movement & Tracking

How They Move

In the water, Loggerheads use their long front flippers for powerful propulsion, resembling "flying" through the water. On land, they move much more laboriously, using an alternating limb movement to drag their heavy bodies across the sand. This creates a distinctive track pattern.

Identifying Footprints

The tracks, often called a "crawl," are characterized by alternating comma-shaped marks from the flippers, with a drag mark from the tail (caruncle) down the center. The track width for an adult female is typically 2.5 to 3 feet.
