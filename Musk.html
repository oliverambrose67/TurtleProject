Flattened Musk Turtle

An Endangered Species of Alabama

Sternotherus depressus

About the Species

Appearance: This is a small turtle, typically 3 to 5 inches long. Its most distinct feature is a very flat upper shell (carapace), which is tan, brown, or nearly black. The head and neck have a unique pattern of dark lines and spots.

Diet: They are specialized eaters (molluscivores), primarily feeding on snails and clams. Their powerful jaws are adapted for crushing shells. They also eat insects and other small invertebrates.

Conservation Status: The Flattened Musk Turtle is listed as Threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. It's endemic to Alabama, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world.

Habitat & Range in Alabama

Habitat: They require clean, clear, free-flowing streams and rivers with sandy or rocky bottoms. The most critical habitat feature is an abundance of submerged rocks, boulders, or logs with crevices. These spaces provide essential shelter from predators and fast currents.

Range: This turtle has one of the most restricted ranges of any turtle in North America. It is found exclusively in the upper Black Warrior River Basin in north-central Alabama. This includes parts of Blount, Cullman, Etowah, Jefferson, Marshall, Walker, and Winston counties.

Nesting Habits

A related musk turtle species nesting. Nesting sites look similar.

Nesting Season: Females typically lay their eggs from late May to early July.

Nest Location: Nests are dug in loose, well-drained soil, sand, or piles of decaying leaves and bark. They are usually located close to the water's edge, often in areas that receive direct sunlight to help incubate the eggs.

Clutch Size: They have a very low reproductive rate, laying only one or two small, hard-shelled eggs per clutch. A female might lay more than one clutch per season.

Movement & Tracking Data

Locomotion: This turtle is primarily a "bottom walker." It crawls along the riverbed, probing for food and moving between shelter rocks. They are not strong, open-water swimmers and rarely bask out of the water. Their flat shape helps them navigate currents and squeeze into tight crevices.

Footprints for Tracking: Finding clear footprints is rare due to their rocky habitat. However, in sandy or muddy shallows, their tracks would show:

  • Four distinct foot impressions.
  • Each foot has five toes with visible claw marks at the tips.
  • A central drag mark may be visible from the tail and lower shell (plastron) scraping the ground.
  • The stride is short and alternating, creating a shuffling pattern.
