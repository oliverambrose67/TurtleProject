Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

Lepidochelys kempii

Welcome! This presentation provides key information about the world's most endangered sea turtle. We will explore its biology, habitat, and why it's a critical species to protect, especially here in Alabama.

About the Species

  • Smallest Sea Turtle: They weigh 75-100 pounds and are about 2 feet long.
  • Appearance: They have a distinctive, almost circular, grayish-green top shell (carapace).
  • Diet: Carnivores that eat crabs, jellyfish, and mollusks, using powerful jaws to crush shells.
  • Status: Critically Endangered due to threats like bycatch in fishing gear and habitat loss.

Habitat & Alabama Location

Primary Habitat

Kemp's Ridleys prefer the shallow, coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico with sandy or muddy bottoms for foraging.

Location in Alabama

They are found in Alabama's coastal waters, including Mobile Bay. Bays and estuaries are important feeding grounds for young turtles.

Nesting Habits: The "Arribada"

  • Unique Nesting: Famous for "arribadas," where thousands of females nest together during the day.
  • Primary Nesting Beach: Over 95% of nesting occurs on one beach in Rancho Nuevo, Mexico.
  • Nesting in the US: Some nesting occurs on the Texas coast, with rare, isolated nests found in other Gulf states.
  • Process: Females lay about 100 eggs in a dug nest. The eggs incubate for 45-60 days.

Movement & Tracking

Movement & Tracks

In water, they use front flippers for power. On land, they use an alternating gait, creating a distinctive track pattern perfect for AI model training.

  • Tracks are symmetrical with alternating flipper marks.
  • A central drag mark is left by the tail.
  • The total track width is typically 28-35 inches.
